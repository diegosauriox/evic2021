¿Qué es EVIC?

La Escuela Latinoamericana de Verano de Inteligencia Computacional (EVIC) es un evento anual que se realiza en Chile desde el año 2004. Su objetivo es promover la Inteligencia Computacional, reuniendo áreas como Control Inteligente, Robótica, Visión por Computadora, Neurociencia Computacional, Ciencia de Datos, Reconocimiento de patrones y Big Data entre otros. En el evento se presentan desde los conceptos básicos hasta los últimos resultados de la investigación en estas áreas. Tiene como público objetivo estudiantes de pregrado y posgrado, profesore(a)s universitarios y profesionales, tanto de Chile como de países Latinoamericanos. EVIC está organizada por el Capítulo Chileno del la Sociedad de Inteligencia Computacional de la IEEE (Computational Intelligence Society, IEEE-CIS) y la Universidad anfitriona. EVIC es un evento que nació en la Universidad de Chile, pero para lograr un mayor alcance y diversidad de sus asistentes, desde el año 2013 se ha vuelto itinerante, realizándose en distintas universidades del país, tales como la Universidad de La Frontera (EVIC 2013), la Universidad de Concepción (EVIC 2015), la Universidad de Valparaíso (EVIC 2017), la Universidad de Santiago (EVIC2018) y la Universidad Austral (EVIC 2019). Este año vuelve a Temuco por segunda vez.

La Inteligencia Computacional (IC) es un área de la Ingeniería que está teniendo un fuerte impacto en prácticamente todas las áreas del conocimiento, ya que provee herramientas para automatizar el análisis de la información y crear sistemas capaces de tomar decisiones autónomas. Estas tecnologías impactarán fuertemente en la sociedad, incidiendo fuertemente en la economía y en la vida cotidiana de las personas, a la vez que ofrecen oportunidades para crear nuevos polos de desarrollo para la región. Como en años anteriores, hemos invitado a reconocidos conferencistas que podrán presentar sus trabajos y aportes en diversas líneas y aplicaciones de la IC. El programa de EVIC 2020 contempla Tutoriales y Charlas Plenarias. Además, debido a la pandemia que nos afecta, este evento será en línea y gratuito para sus participantes.

Los y las invitamos entonces muy cordialmente a estar atentos y atentas a los plazos de inscripción para que puedan participar de este interesante evento. Muy cordialmente.

ORADORES DE LAS PLENARIAS

Dr. Felipe Tobar

Title: Bayesian modelling of multi-channel time series: a Python toolbox

The unified analysis of multiple time series is commonplace in several areas of science and engineering, where multivariate signals arise from electrodes in EEG, stocks in finance, climate observations in sensor networks and mineral concentrations in geology. In particular, statistical multivariate analysis is strongly needed due to missing and corrupted data, however, it is also challenging, since we need to model data dependency both in time and across space (or channels). To address the problem of statistical analysis of multiple time series, we present the multi-output Gaussian (MOGP) process framework, which models signals from a Bayesian nonparametric perspective. We will complement the MOGP rationale with an applied demonstration based on our recently introduced toolbox MOGPTK, which uses a Python front-end, relies on the GPflow suite and it is built on a TensorFlow back-end, thus enabling GPU-accelerated training. As we will see, the toolkit facilitates implementing the entire pipeline of GP modelling, including data loading, parameter initialization, model learning, parameter interpretation, up to data imputation and extrapolation.

The talk will be based on the following works (model and toolkit respectively):

  • https://papers.nips.cc/paper/7245-spectral-mixture-kernels-for-multi-output-gaussian-processes
  • https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.03471

    Dr. Piero Bonissone

    Title: Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) Analytics for Industrial Internet (aka Industrial AI)

    The Industrial Internet is the third disruptive wave, after the Industrial and the Internet revolutions. It is transforming our industries, just like the Internet revolution transformed our commerce. In this new context, we face a combination of hyper-connected intelligent machines, interacting with other machines and people, and generating large amounts data that need to be analyzed by descriptive, predictive, and prescriptive models. As a result, we see the resurgence of analytics as a key differentiator for creating new services, of cloud computing as an enabling technology for service delivery, and of crowdsourcing as a new phenomenon in which people play critical roles in creating information and shaping decisions in a variety of problems. We explore the intersection of these three concepts from the perspective of a machine-learning researcher and show how his job and roles have evolved over time. In the past, analytic model creation was an artisanal process, as models were handcrafted by experienced, knowledgeable model-builders. More recently, the use of meta-heuristics, such as evolutionary algorithms, has provided us with limited levels of automation in model building and maintenance. In the short future, we expect data-driven analytic models to become a commodity. We envision having access to a large number of data-driven models, obtained by a combination of crowdsourcing, cloud-based evolutionary algorithms, outsourcing, in-house development, and legacy models. In this context, the critical issue will be model ensemble selection and fusion, rather than model generation. First, we will review the application of data-driven analytic models to assets diagnostics and prognostics, (PHM) such as aircraft engines, medical imaging devices, and locomotives. We will cover case studies on anomaly detection, diagnosis, prognostics, and optimization. Then we will describe the evolution of analytic models with the advent of cloud computing, and propose the use of customized model ensembles on demand, inspired by Lazy Learning. This approach is agnostic with respect to the origin of the models, making it scalable and suitable for a variety of applications. We successfully tested this approach in a regression problem for a power plant management application, using two different sources of models: bootstrapped neural networks, and GP-created symbolic regression models evolved in the cloud. We will also present results on the fusion of models for FlyQuest, a GE-sponsored Kaggle competition in which we crowd-sourced the generation of models predicting the estimated runway and gateway arrival (ERA, EGA) over a month of US flights. Finally, we will highlight research trends, challenges and opportunities for Machine Learning techniques in this emerging context of big data and cloud computing.

    Dra. Carla Vairetti/Dr. Sebastián Maldonado

    Title: How Computational Intelligence is changing the Customer Experience

    Customer experience is a hot topic in business analytics. To gain a competitive advantage on a global scale, it is necessary to focus strongly on the customer. Technology development and investment has shown the importance of developing state of the art solutions to enhance the understanding of the customer. Companies that do this are 6% more profitable and 5% more productive than others on average. The interaction between a company and its customers has increased in the last years and, with it, the collection of data from these interactions. Customer feedback is a rich source of information about the perception of a company, but it is difficult to understand the hidden information that is behind the reviews and relate it with key areas of the business. In this talk we present a framework for extracting valuable knowledge from customer feedback data using AI models, such as BERT, and other natural language processing techniques. This strategy not only supports automatic analysis of new data but also provides important insights into the applications. Experiments carried out using 25,943 customer survey responses related to 39 service companies in 13 different economic sectors demonstrate the virtues of the approach.

    Dra. Alice Smith

    Tittle: Evolutionary Strategies for Difficult Engineering Design Problems

    This presentation will put forth several straightforward but successful implementations of an often overlooked evolutionary algorithm – evolutionary strategies, ES – for the design of complex systems. ES was developed more than 50 years ago for optimizing engineering design problems in continuous space and is characterized by its simplicity and computational efficiency. There are few tunable parameters in the basic version and the search relies on the evolution of a population through mutation only, where mutation is a Gaussian which adapts automatically to the search history. Such simplicity is appealing for both algorithm development and implementation and tends to result in a robust search. The three engineering design problems showcased in this talk are diverse and two of them are bi-objective optimized to identify a Pareto set of non-dominated designs. The applications are (1) the design of an airfoil for a flying drone considering drag and lift, (2) the location of semi-obnoxious facilities in municipalities considering transport costs and social costs, and (3) the design of large order picking warehouses considering travel distance.

    Dr. Carlos Coello

    Title: Recent Research Topics in Evolutionary Multiobjective Optimization. A Personal Perspective

    In this talk, some research topics that are worth exploring (from the personal perspective of the speaker) in evolutionary multi-objective optimization will be briefly discussed. Such topics include scalability of multi-objective evolutionary algorithms (both in objective and in decision variable space), indicator-based selection, hyper-heuristics, parallelism and scalarizing functions. In the final part of the talk, some personal thoughts about the future of the field will also be briefly discussed.

    Dr. Pablo Huijse

    Title: Deep probabilistic models with applications in astronomy

    In this talk we will review the basics of inference with probabilistic models, the more recent "deep" probabilistic models and how to implement them using the Pyro probabilistic programming library. After that we will have a hands-on experience training probabilistic models to analyze time series from astronomical survey projects.
